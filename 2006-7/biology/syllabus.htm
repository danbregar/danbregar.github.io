Course Description:

Biology is an introductory course that focuses on fundamental concepts in biology.  The following basic biological themes will be covered; nature of science and biology, ecology, biochemistry, the cell, genetics, microbiology, evolution, and biology’s connection to other disciplines and the world.  In addition to conceptual learning, emphasis will be placed on critical analysis of scientific work, project design, collection and interpretation of data, and communication through technical writing and presentations.  Throughout the year we will analyze issues in bioethics, and our roles and responsibilities as knowledgeable people in the scientific and larger world.

 

Instructional Goals:

 At the conclusion of this course, students should have a broad understanding of the field of biology and be prepared to continue with more specific study in the biological (or other) sciences.  More importantly, students should have an understanding of the current issues in the field of biology and be prepared to make educated decisions as local and global citizens. 

 

Textbook:

Biology Miller and Levine (Prentice-Hall 3rd edition).

 

Notebook:

For organization of class lecture notes, daily assignments, and documentation of laboratory work students will be required to keep a spiral (bound, single subject) notebook.  These notebooks will be collected and scored intermittently throughout the year. 

 

Participation:

Labs, group-work, and learning opportunities involving special guests are an important part of this class.  Many of these types of experiences can not be recreated.  Be in class!

 

Seminars:

In small groups, you will research a “hot topic” in the field of biology and lead a comprehensive class discussion on the topic.  A presentation of all sides of the issue is required.  Seminars are generally held on Fridays, beginning roughly the third or fourth Friday of the quarter.  See the seminar document for further information.

 

Grading:

Grades will be assigned based on an average of lab-work, class-work, home-work, and exams.  Students will have opportunities to meet writing, speaking, and scientific inquiry standards.  Ongoing scores will be available online at the web-grade address listed above.

 

Attendance:

To succeed in this class, you will need to attend regularly.  I expect you to be on time every day.  Students who are chronically late or absent will be placed on an attendance contract that will be strictly enforced.

 

Late Work:

If you miss class due to an excused absence, you have one week to make up any assignments you have missed without losing points.  Assignments missed due to an unexcused absence, or work submitted more than one week after an excused absence will receive a zero.  No late work will be graded during the last week of the quarter (this includes tests and quizzes), except for students who miss class due to an excused absence during the last two weeks of the quarter.  Any student who does not complete all assigned work – late or not -  in this class will either receive a no grade (if he or she is passing the class) or an F (if he or she is failing the class).  Students who receive a no grade must submit their missing work within 90 days of the end of the quarter.

 

Behavior:

I expect students in Field Biology to manage their behavior and conduct themselves as adults.  Students who disrupt the learning environment will be placed on a behavior contract and/or removed from class.  Please follow all guidelines on the class safety sheet, and teacher instructions.  Cheating and plagiarism are prohibited and will result in parent notification and disciplinary action.  Students are expected to stay in the classroom during the instructional period.  Extended departures can affect your grade in the class.  Extended leaves (beyond 10 minutes) will result in an unexcused absence for the block.

 

Due to safety issues food is not allowed in the science area unless it is a designated special occasion. 

 

Most importantly, show respect for yourself, others, and the environment.  Biology class should be an enjoyable endeavor.  The less frequently the teacher needs to discipline individuals and class as a whole, the more fun class will be for all involved.
