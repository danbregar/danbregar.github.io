Mitosis

  1. What kinds of cells undergo mitosis? Somatic cells (body cells)
  2. What are the purposes of mitosis or mitotic division? For growth, asexual reproduction, or repair of damaged body cells
  3. If a mutation occurs during mitosis, or on a cell that divides using mitosis, can that mutation be inherited by the organism’s offspring? Explain. No. The mutation occurs in a body cell or somatic cell. Only gametes or sex cells contribute to the future genes and development of an offspring. The only way the mutation could be inherited is if the organism reproduced asexually!
  4. How many daughter cells are produced during mitosis?2 genetically identical daughter cells
  5. How do the daughter cells of mitosis compare to the parent cell (genetically)? They are genetically identical.
  6. Describe three differences between mitosis and meiosis. Number of daughter cells produced: Mitosis (2), Meiosis (4)

Number and type of divisions of genetic material (mitosis-1 division of sister chromatids, meiosis-2 divisions; 1st the homologous chromosome pairs, then the sister chromatids)

Heritability of mutations (if a cell mutates prior to or during meiosis the mutation can be passed on, this is not the case in mitosis unless the mitotic division in an asexual organism).

  1. What are traits, genes, and alleles? (vocabulary is your responsibility, I recommend drawing a picture for this one)
  2. What are Punnett squares used for? (1)to predict the possible phenotypes and genotypes of offspring that result from a cross of two parents, (2) to model the link between the processes of meiosis and fertilization in heredity, (3) to show the probability of inheritance of specific traits)
  3. What 2 processes do Punnett squares model? (meiosis and fertilization)
  4. Describe the principle of independent assortment and the principle of segregation discovered by Mendel.

Mendel's Law of Segregation states that the 2 alleles (versions) of each gene pair separate into different gametes (egg or sperm) and do so randomly. A parent has 2 possible versions of a trait (alleles).  The allele that gets donated to an individual gamete is a matter of chance (like a coin flip). Therefore, alleles are said to segregate independently. The version of a gene (allele) inherited from a parent is a matter of chance and this segregation of alleles (each version riding on one of two homologous chromosomes) occurs during meiosis.

 

Mendel’s Law of Independent Assortment: Mendel found out that individual alleles segregate randomly into gametes, but he was only looking at one trait’s alleles at a time when he discovered the law of segregation of alleles. His next step was to see if this pattern was similar when looking at multiple traits at a time. Did the law of segregation hold true for traits on 2 separate chromosomes or do the patterns of inheritance on one chromosome govern the patterns of inheritance (which allele a gamete gets) on another?

Genes that segregate independently (for example the genes for seed shape and plant height) do not influence each other’s inheritance. This is often because the genes that display this behavior are located on separate chromosomes. Genes for different traits can segregate (separate) independently during the formation of gametes (meiosis). Independent assortment helps account for the many genetic variations observed in sexually reproducing plants, animals, and other organisms.
