Punnett Squares in 5 steps

 

  1.  Identify the traits being studied and choose a letter to represent the genes in the cross.
  2. Write the genotypes of the parents (Capitol letters for dominant alleles lowercase for recessive).
  3. Determine the possible gametes (haploid reproductive cells) that parents can produce.
  4. Enter the possible gametes at the top and side of the Punnett square.
  5. Complete the Punnett square by writing the alleles from the gametes in the corresponding boxes.

 
 
 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


Example Problem: What Happens when you mix Meatballs and Nachos?

 

    • Suppose that Meatball and Nacho are purebred cats.
    • Nacho is homozygous dominant for the absence of “thumbs”
    • Meatball is homozygous recessive for the absence of “thumbs” (in other words, Meatball has thumbs).

The letters inside the yellow boxes represents all the possibilities for trait inheritance in offspring as a result of this cross. So, the yellow boxes represent fertilization of any two possible haploid gametes (sex cells) donated from the parents in meiosis!

 

For this cross we get 4 heterozygotes (Aa). The genotypes produced are all Aa.

The phenotype expressed would be absence of thumbs (or thumbless cats) because all the offspring carry the dominant allele for thumblessness from Nacho.
