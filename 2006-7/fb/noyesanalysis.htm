To analyze the data you collected at the Noyes property, you will use a statistical test called a “Chi-Squared Analysis”.  I do not expect you to understand the mathematics behind this test!  However, I do expect that you will be able to compile the data we collected, arrange it in the proper format, and interpret the results of the Chi-Squared Analysis.

 

To begin with, copy the data we collected from the spreadsheet posted on my web site to a new, blank spreadsheet.  This data is arranged so that at every gridpoint, species that we observed are marked with a “1” and species that were absent are marked with a “0”.  To correctly interpret this data, you will need to follow these steps:

 

1.   Reorder the data so that all the gridpoints that contained poison oak are in one location and all the gridpoints without poison oak are in another location.

2.   Find the average “presence or absence” value of each plant – you will end up with two averages for each plant; one for poison oak gridpoints and one for non-poison oak gridpoints.

4.   To perform a Chi-Squared analysis, select a blank cell on your worksheet.  Click on the “Fx” (function) button below the toolbar.  From the “Statistics” category, choose “CHITEST”.

5.   For the “Actual Range” select the second set of averages (for gridpoints without poison oak).  For the “Expected Range” select the first set of averages (for gridpoints with poison oak).

6.   To interpret your Chi-Squared test, use the following guidelines:

a.   If the result of your Chi-Squared test is 0.05 or less, then there was a statistically significant difference between the plants present with poison oak and the plants present in the absence of poison oak.

b.   If the results of your Chi-Squared test is more than 0.05, then it is possible that any differences in your averages are merely due to chance and do not indicate that there is a real difference in the composition of plants in areas with poison oak and areas without poison oak.
