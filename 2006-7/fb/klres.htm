Results and Analysis Guidelines – Kincaid’s Lupine Project

Field Biology

 

This is the format for the last part of your Kincaid’s Lupine Project.  This report will include the data that was collected at Bald Hill on 4/11/07 (this data is posted on the website).  This section will also include a discussion of that data in an attempt to answer the question:  “What is the relationship between different soil types and the health / growth of Kincaid’s Lupine?”.

 

This is an individual assignment – you might need to discuss the data with others in the class, but your final report should reflect your own work and your own thinking.  A well-written report should be between 4 – 5 pages, including tables and graphs.

 

Please include the following sections in your report.  Include section headings so that each section is easy to find.

 

Results:

This section should include a verbal and numeric summary of the data posted on the web site.  Where appropriate, calculate average values for the measurements and create a graph or chart for your data.

 

Your data should take the form of both RAW data (the original measurements and calculations that appear on the spreadsheet) and COMPILED data (smaller spreadsheets that show only average values and other calculations).  Among your tables, you should create a data table that shows the average number of leaves, height, and leaf width for the plants in each of the two different soil types we investigated.  (There should be a graph for this table as well).  You should have a total of at least 3 different tables and 3 different graphs.

 

Along with your data tables, this section should include a written description of the results.  You do not need to characterize the observations for each plant, but you should summarize what each table and graph shows in a sentence or two.

 

Discussion:

This section is the most important part of your report.  You should draw conclusions and generalizations from the data, point out any errors or exceptions that might have occurred, and discuss any practical and theoretical implications of the work.  Because we have a limited number of plants and experimental setups, we cannot say that we have conclusively proved anything we’ve looked at.  Instead, the data we’ve collected will simply provide evidence for a position.  As a result, all of your statements should indicate this uncertainty whenever possible.  For example, instead of saying “…our results prove that non-native plants require more nutrients than native plants”, write “…our results indicate that non-native plants might require more nutrients than native plants.”

 

In your discussion, make sure that you address the following points:

a.   What, according to your data, was the answer to your question?  In other words, was your hypothesis correct?

b.   Why did you get the results you did?  Your goal is to explain the scientific causes that might generate the results you saw.  You cannot be certain about the reasons you propose in answer to this question; instead, each reason you suggest might be the basis for a future scientific experiment.  You should list between at least 3 possible reasons that explain why you obtained the results you did.  For example, if you find that the plants grown in the Rhizobium + Bi Mart soil mixture grow taller than the other plants, you should speculate as to WHY you see this relationship.  It should be possible for your ideas to be tested experimentally; these are the sorts of ideas that might serve as the basis for a future project.

c.   What could you and the rest of the class have done differently to improve your project?  In other words, what advice would you give to future groups if they wanted to duplicate or extend this study?

d.   What are the next logical steps for this study?  In other words, if you had another semester to continue working on this project, what types of studies or experiments would you set up?  How would these studies or experiments help you to understand the relationship between the factors we are studying?
