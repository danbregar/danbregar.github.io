The first one is a prairie soil and a Bi-Mart soil mixture, and the second one is Bi-Mart soil but we dipped the roots rhizobium, so the plants are able to get more nitrogen.

 b. Kincaid’s Lupine is a native endangered plant species. Native plants are extremely important to our ecosystem. They provide an excellent source of food and shelter for birds and animals. They can survive under intense conditions (Wallace W. Hansen 2005). The roots from native plants help hold soil and slow down erosion. Migrating birds, threatened and endangered species have used native plants as food and shelter. And fish eat the organisms that feed on twigs dropped by native plants in streams (“Guide for Using Willamette Valley…” 1998).

There are also butterfly species that will eat only native plants and some that will only eat one variety of native plants. Native plants are easier for the butterflies and insects to eat rather than plants brought in from somewhere other than the Willamette Valley because native plants are already adapted to our climate (B. Newhouse 2001).

Using non-native plants can cause problems for Oregon’s environment. Birds, wind, animals, and humans commonly spread non-native plants. They are often brought in to the Willamette Valley for landscaping and agriculture use. But they often escape and override the native plants and cause massive harm to our already existing plant areas. This can clog waterways, degrade recreational areas, lower land values, and some are even poisonous (Invasive Plants 2002).

Non-native plants can invade an area and wipe out native plants that were living there before; they can also genetically alter a native plant. (Native Plants vs. Non-native Plants).

 

 

 

Oak Savannahs have become rare in the Willamette Valley. Some due to residential growth, cities are expanding physically and the savannahs are decreasing. Agricultural purposes also play a factor in the rarity of the land; more and more land is used to grow crops than kept to preserve it. Native and non-native plants and weeds have become a problem and are threatening the habitat of the oak savannahs.

Fire suppression, which is good for preventing forest fires but also destroys the savannah environment (Pak Kin Chan 2004). They all play a part to why oak savannahs are becoming more and more rare.

Some advantages of burning oak savannahs are that it keeps them healthy and strong. It also keeps the trees and shrubs from spreading too rapidly (Minnesota Department 2006). It has allowed habitat restoration especially for the endangered environment. Burning increases a diversity of plants and the quality and quantity of the habitat (Texas Parks and Wildlife Department 2006). Some cons are that the fire could get out of hand and burn more than wanted and expected.

 The Fender’s Blue Butterfly, once thought to be extinct, only eats Kincaid’s Lupine (Oregon Zoo 2005). There less than 1000 acres of habitat left that the Fender’s Blue Butterfly and Kincaid’s Lupine and survive in (US Fish and Wildlife Service). The Butterfly Species are completely dependent on the survival of the Kincaid’s Lupine plant (Wikipedia encyclopedia 2006). And if this habitat completely disappears then the Fender’s Blue Butterfly will not have anything to feed off of and go extinct completely.

Nitrogen is essential for all plants to live. However plants can’t fix the nitrogen, meaning combining it with hydrogen and turning it to ammonia, alone (Wikipedia encyclopedia 2006). Rhizobium is soil bacteria that live on the plants and fix nitrogen so the plants can survive. But the rhizobium also needs a plant host in order to fix the nitrogen. On the plants, they leave nodules where the nitrogen was being fixed.

The rhizobium feed the plants the necessary nitrogen for it to survive. This means they need to rely less on outside nitrogen fertilizers (Dr. Mitchell Andrews). Because of the rhizobium, plants can exist in poor, non-ideal living conditions. They can help improve the survival and growth of the plant and reduce the cost (Agroforestry Net, Inc 2006).
