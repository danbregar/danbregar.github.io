To The Doom!  Data Analysis

Field Biology

 

New observations from the game:

1.   The numbers at the bottom show how many dots of each color are left.

2.   The dots all started with eight.

3.   Some of the dots end up less than eight, some end up more than eight – so dots are being added.

4.   Most of the time, the dot that most closely matched the background color was the most abundant at the end of the game (see data).

5.   If dot selection were truly random (uninfluenced by background color) on average, the same number of dots of each color would be left.

 

SO:

1.   In pairs, come up with a “model” – your best guess as to what the rules of the game are.  Your model should account for all of the observations above in addition to any observations you might have made.  Remember, you don’t know how the game really works – and there’s no way for you to find out.  Your model should take the form of a list of proposed rules that explain what we’ve observed in the game.  Here is an example (that wouldn’t really work, by the way):

a.   Each time a dot is clicked on, another dot disappears of the same color

b.   If the dot clicked on is the same as the background color, two random dots will disappear as well

c.   When two dots in a row of the same color are clicked, a dot of that color will reappear

 

2.   Design at least two tests for your model – your tests should allow you to see if your rules are correct.  You should design as many tests as necessary to test your rules – one will almost certainly not be enough.  Here is an example of a test for rule (a) above:

a.   Click on as many dots as possible for thirty seconds to clear the playfield

b.   Try to reduce at least one type of dot to just two or three

c.   Click on a dot and see if another dot of the same color disappears

 

3.   Test your model!  Take notes on your observations – if your tests appear to validate a rule, put a checkmark by it.  If your tests indicate that your rule is wrong, cross it out.

 

4.   Revise your rules based on your new data – repeat #’s 1, 2, and 3.
