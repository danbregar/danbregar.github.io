Revisiting antimicrobial resistance

 

Antimicrobial resistance occurs when a microbe (a really small organism) is not killed by a substance that usually kills microbes of that type.  For example, antibiotics are chemicals that are used to kill bacteria.  When some bacteria are not killed by an antibiotic, they are said to have developed antibiotic resistance.

 

Think about antibiotic resistance the same way that you might think of an immunity to poison oak.  Most people are allergic to poison oak, just like most bacteria are killed by antibiotics.  However, some people are not affected by poison oak – just like some bacteria (that are antibiotic resistance) are not affected by antibiotics.

 

The graphs that we looked at the other day showed how in some tests, some organisms appear to be becoming more resistant to antibiotics over time.  The particular organism looked at is a bacteria called Salmonella.  Over a period of several years, Salmonella bacteria that were extracted from humans, cattle, and other organisms showed an increased ability to survive an application of an antibiotic.

 

This would be similar to a situation in which over a long period of time (50 – 100 years), people who lived in Corvallis appeared to become more and more immune to poison oak – for example, if in 1950, 10% of the population were immune to poison oak, and in 2007, 25% of the population were immune to poison oak.

 

Discussion question 1:

What property of a bacteria might give it the ability to resist antibiotics?  Note that we are not trying to determine how antibiotic resistance might come about over time in a population of bacteria – we are trying to imagine what physical property a bacteria might possess that would allow it to survive an antibiotic application.  This would be like asking what is unique about Mr. Bregar that allows him to come into contact with poison oak without developing a rash.

 

We know that like all other organisms, bacteria reproduce and die.  This means that in 1996, the bacteria that were tested in the government study we looked at were not the same exact individuals that were tested in 2003.  So if the number of antibiotic resistant bacteria is increasing over time, whatever property is allowing these bacteria to survive must also be spreading.

 

Discussion question 2:

Take your answer to discussion question 1 – how might the property you’ve described spread through a population over a period of several years?  Keep in mind that since these organisms are reproducing and dying, somehow this property must be occurring in new individuals.  This would be like asking how immunity to poison oak could spread through the population of Corvallis over 50 years – keeping in mind that like in any other city, Corvallis residents are moving in and out, having kids, and passing away.

 

Your description of how antibiotic resistance can spread through a population over time is an example of a “model” – a description of how something might happen – even if you aren’t sure that it is completely accurate.  In order to be considered a scientific model, it needs to meet the criteria we discussed (and a few others) the other day when we talked about what science actually is:

 

a.   It should be based on observable evidence (empirical)

b.   It should demonstrate repeatability – for example, it should explain how antibiotic resistance might arise in a different population of bacteria.

c.   It should effectively predict what kinds of changes (or lack thereof) we might see in bacteria populations in the future

d.   It should adequately explain antibiotic resistance and how it could change in a population

e.   It should be relatively simple

f.    It should rely solely upon previously existing scientific ideas or principles

g.   It should be testable – and potentially disprovable (but not provable)

h.   It should be possible to modify your model to account for new observations
