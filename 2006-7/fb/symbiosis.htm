SYMBIOSIS TERMS

 

MUTUALISM: Both partners benefit from the association

 

Examples:

 

·        Ambrosia beetles cultivating ambrosia fungus for their larvae

·        Rhizobium bacteria and roots of Kincade Lupine and other legumes such as peas and beans

·        Mycorrhizal roots and fungi.  The roots bring in sugars produced by photosynthesis in the trees and the fungus gets the sugars in the roots.  The fungus brings in essential minerals needed by the tree to live.

·        Mycorrhizal fungi and mammals.  The fungi produce truffles with spores underground.  The mammals collect the truffles and move them to their nests.  The fungus benefits by having its spores moved from one point to another underground, and the spores then germinating near tree roots.  The mammal benefits by getting food from the truffle.

 

COMMENSALISM: One partner benefits, the other is unaffected.

 

Example:

 

·        Fungi which travel on the backs of beetles from one log to another.  The beetle is unaffected by the fungus, but the fungus gets a free ride to a new food source.

 

PARASITISM: One partner benefits, the other is harmed.

 

Example:

 

·        Fender’s blue butterfly and Kincade’s Lupine.  The butterfly larvae hatch from eggs and eat the leaves of the Kincade’s Lupine.  The butterfly larvae benefit, and the Lupine is harmed by the larvae.
