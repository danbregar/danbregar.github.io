Research Tips

 

When conducting background research for a scientific investigation, it helps to have a framework to give you some direction.  These tips are intended to apply to a variety of different types of research projects – so you may need to think of some more specific ways to address certain topics.

 

1.   The main question you are trying to address is “What scientific knowledge exists about my topic?”.  Avoid including trivia or highly specific information unless it is pertinent to your overall project.

2.   Here are some general questions you can target in your research:

For Plants:

Where does it grow?

What type of climate does it prefer?

What type of soil does it prefer?

How much moisture does it need?

How does it reproduce?

What animals or insects eat it?

How is it used by humans?

Describe other relevant scientific studies that have been done on this plant?

For Animals:

Where does it live?

What types of habitat does it prefer?

How and when does it reproduce?

What eats it?  What does it eat?

How does it interact with the soil and/or water it inhabits?

Describe other relevant scientific studies that have been done on this animal?

For Soil, Water, Air, or other abiotic factors:

How does it affect plants and animals?

How does it affect the local environment (microclimate)?

How does it change at different times or in different spots?

What is its physical and chemical composition?

Describe other relevant scientific studies that have been done on this factor.

3.   Before beginning your search, make a list of different search terms you can use – the questions above will help you come up with some good search terms.

4.   Find the scientific names of organisms you are researching and use those names in your search in addition to the common name.

5.   If you are investigating a correlation between two things, research each separately in addition to looking for information relating to your overall question.

6.   When doing an internet search, start with Google.  There are other ways to find information, but Google tends to do a very good job of locating appropriate sources.

7.   When you find something interesting, the FIRST thing you should do is collect all of the information you will need for a citation.

8.   When using a computer, keep a Microsoft Word document open at all times.  When you find something interesting on-line, copy and paste it into your Word document along with all of the citation information.  Then, you can go back later and paraphrase it.  Always remember to include the URL (web address) so you can find the information later on if necessary.

9.   Take notes on your search results. You may see an author’s name referenced in a journal abstract – copy this name down and search on it later. You may also see key words in the titles, subjects, or literature cited of journal articles that relate to your topics – again, copy this information down and search on it later.
