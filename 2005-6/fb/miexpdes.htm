Experimental Design for Aquatic

Macroinvertebrate Inquiry Project

Field Biology

 

Last week, you developed a list of possible questions that you could answer for this project.  Today, you will decide which question to address, determine how to collect data for this project, and create a data sheet you can use to write your information down.

 

Here are the steps you should follow:

1.   (5 minutes) With your partner, review the questions you came up with last week.  Pick one of these questions – make sure it is one that you are interested in, and make sure to think carefully about whether it is a question that can be answered by collecting data for roughly one hour.

 

2.   (15 minutes) You already have an established procedure for collecting macroinvertebrates (posted on my website).  With your partner, come up with a step-by-step list of methods that you can use to make the other measurements you will need to do for this project.  Your methods should be clear and extremely detailed – they should allow anyone to go out, make the same measurements you do, and get the exact same results that you do.  For example, if you are making a measurement of plant height, do not simply write “measure plant height”.  What units will you use?  If the plant has many stems, which one will they choose?  Will they straighten out the stem before measuring it?  Your instructions should specify exactly how these measurements will be made.

 

3.   (5 – 10 minutes) Have two other groups of students review your question and your methods to make sure that your question is reasonable and understandable and that your methods are clear, specific, and detailed.  Make any necessary changes.

 

4.   (20 minutes) Make a data sheet (or data sheets) that you can use to collect the data for your project.  You might want to have two – one for the macroinvertebrates, and one for the other measurements you will make.  Make sure your data sheet has a spot for every piece of information you will need to measure or calculate.  Also make sure your data sheet has a spot for your names, the dates, the location from which data was collected, sample numbers/ID’s, and any other relevant information.  Make your data sheet neat, clean, and legible – use a ruler to make gridlines, and leave plenty of room to write all the information you’ll need.

 

5.   (5 – 10 minutes) Have two other groups of students review your data sheet(s) and make sure they have all the information you’ll need to answer your question.  Make any necessary changes.

 

At the end of the period, make sure that the instructor checks off both your written methods and your data sheet!
