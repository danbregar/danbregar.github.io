Soil Terms and Concepts

 

Alluvium.  Graded material that was deposited by rivers or streams. 

The soils of the Willamette Valley are largely alluvial material that tends to

be largely silt loam materials.  Alluvial soils are typically well graded and

stratified (the layers alternate) with silts and clays in low lying areas along

rivers and with sandy soils located on slightly higher areas called terraces.

 

Lacustrine.  Graded material that was deposited by lakes.  Sands are

on the outside of the lakelaid soils and can be viewed as former lake beaches. 

Clays settled in the direct center of the former lake.  Silty soils are located

between the clays and the beach sands.

 

Residuum.  Soil that developed in place from the bedrock below. 

Occurs commonly in mountainous areas.  Oregon residuum tends to be shallow,

a foot or two deep.

 

Glacial Till.  Mixed material (mixture of sand, silt, and clay) that was

deposited by glaciers.  Usually sandy loam, loam, clay loam textures.

 

Colluvium.  Material that has moved downward through erosion.

 

Loess.  Wind blown soil that tends to be silt loam or very fine

sandy loam texture.  Important in eastern Oregon and Washington.  Loess soils

tend to erode easily.

Russian Factors of Soil Formation.  Time, Topography, Climate, Vegetation, and Parent Material.  These five factors serve to form soil as we see it today. 

Soil Texture:  refers to the distribution of sand, silt, and clay

Textural triangle:  allows us to give a soil a soil texture (ex  sandy loam, silt loam, etc) based on the % of sand, list, and clay.

Organic Matter = humus, decomposed plant and animal material (often dead undecomposed plant material is included as organic matter as well)

Micropores:  small pores that are found in fine textured soils like clays

Macropores: large pores that are found in coarse textured soil like sands, gravels

Atmospheric and Soil Air  are composed of Nitrogen, Oxygen, Carbon Dioxide, etc.

Soil Water:  can be either plant available or not available (clays will contain water that is held so tightly in micropores that plant roots will not be able to use it.

Leaching:  the movement of a substance vertically in soil.  For instance, calcium and magnesium will leach deep into the soil profile (they will move downward into the soil) in areas with high amounts of rain.

Horizon: horizontal part of the soil profile that has distinctive properties.  For instance, the A horizon is dark colored and contains a great many roots.  Below it is the B horizon.

A horizon:  also called the surface soil.  Most of the biological activities take place in this area of the soil, including roots.

B horizon:  subsoil.  This is a middle area between the A and the C horizons.  There are biological activities that are taking place.  Usually plentiful roots.

C horizon:  underlying material.  Few roots.  Little or no biological activity.

 

Vegetation:  Forested v. Tall Grass Prairie v. Wetland

Forested Soil :  has an O (for Organic) horizon composed of forest litter;  thin dark colored surface soil (A horizon); E horizon of downward clay movement; argillic (Bt) horizon where clay accumulates.  Surface soils tend to be neutral to acid (low pH).

Tall Grass Prairie Soil:  thick, dark surface soil; normal B horizon.  Surface soils tend to be neutral to alkaline (high pH).

Wetland Soil: can be mineral or organic material

Very poorly drained mineral soil:  there is no B horizon.  C horizon is gray color.

Organic soil:  Reed sedge peat (pH  about 5.5); Woody peat (pH  about 5.5); Moss:  Sphagnum sp. (pH  3.5-4.0); Moss: Hypnum sp. (pH >5.5)

Permeability:  refers to the time it takes for a liquid to travel through a medium. Can be viewed in a soil or in a column.

Biological Zero:  microbes (bacteria, fungi) and other soil organisms will not be active at temperatures below about 40 degrees F.   At low temperatures, organic material will tend to build up.  A horizons will be darker and frequently thicker.

 

Stratification:  situation where very different soil layers are next to each other in a soil profile.  Example:  an alluvial soil is formed through frequent flooding of a river channel.  Each time soil material is spilled onto a nearby field.  Due to the varying velocity of the water from one flood to another, the layering will be very different from each flood.  One flood may lay down a layer of fine sand, the next flood may lay down a layer of fine silts.  The material is, however,  graded (separated into similarly sized particles).  Later  in managing this soil that is composed of well graded alluvium  we find that water is slow to penetrate the B and C horizons due to the presence of several layers of stratified sands and silts.  For instance, as water moves easily through a sand layer it encounters a thin one inch layer of silts.  The downward movement of water immediately stops.  It begins to move downward into the silts only as the pore space in the sands above it is almost completely filled with water.  Downward movement of water can be slowed greatly if the stratification is considerable  that is, if the number of layers of different texture is significant.  Stratification can also refer to the layering of geological material.
