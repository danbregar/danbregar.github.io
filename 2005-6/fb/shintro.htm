Field Biology

 

Introduction:

In this section, state what your research question is.  The first sentence should be a brief, concise description of your question (for example, "The objective of this study is to determine the relationship between the arsenic level of soil near Ames Creek and the ratio of native to non-native shrubs").  This should be followed with supporting sentences that add details (for example, "Our goals are to map out the existing distributions of native and non-native shrubs and correlate that information with arsenic content as determined by neutron activation and kit tests").

 

This section should also include background information – several paragraphs that answer the question “why is this study or project worthwhile”?  Answer this question from a scientific point of view - what is the scientific importance of the topics you are covering and what is the scientific relevance of the data you expect to collect?  Think about the following questions when writing your background information:

1.  What can existing scientific research tell you about the factors you are studying?  In other words, what is the ecological significance of the factors you are studying?  For example, why are non-native plants ecologically significant?  What types of chemical combinations of arsenic are commonly found in soil, rocks, and plants?

2.  Is there any pre-existing research that is related to your question?  If so, give a brief summary of the projects that have been done in the past.  Make sure you describe what was done and what was learned from each study.  You probably will not find studies that address the exact question you are looking at, but you will probably discover questions that are similar.

3.  Why might the answer to your question be valuable to scientists?  For example, if you do find a relationship between soil arsenic and the native/non-native shrub ratio, how might that information be used by other ecologists?

Your background information should include citations from your research.  You need to use proper citation format.  Your background information, if done well, should be 3-6 paragraphs long.

 

This section should include a detailed site description and site location.  Give as much information about your site as practical – include a general description of the terrain, likely plant life, and location.  Include maps and aerial photographs (from terraserver.microsoft.com). 

 

Finally, this section should include a hypothesis.  Your hypothesis should be the last thing you do in your introduction and it should be developed from your research.  In other words, use your research you uncover as a basis for your prediction – do not make a prediction and then try to find information that support it.  Your hypothesis should explain what you expect to see and why; for example, "…we expect that non-native shrubs will dominate along Jackson Creek due to human draining of the former wetlands areas along the creek.  Non-native shrubs are generally known to outcompete native shrubs in recently drained areas of the Willamette Valley (Philips, 1997)".  Your statements should be backed by citations from your research – your opinion is not important, it is the methods and opinions of previous researchers that matters.

 

References

You need to include a list of references, in APA format, in alphabetical order.  Any cited sources should appear in your list of references.
