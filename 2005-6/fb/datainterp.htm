Data Interpretation

Field Biology

 

Data Compilation / Graphing:

 

1.   Interview students from the class.  Record the name, gender, and age of five students.  Record the name, gender, age, and height (in inches) of five additional students.  Add the birth month for five additional students.  Add the year in school for five additional students.  Add the eye color of five additional students.

 

2.   Enter your data into a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet.  Put students' names in the left-most column; data fields should run from left to right (see sample spreadsheet).

 

3.   Make a bar graph showing the average age of males vs. females.

 

4.   Make a stacked bar graph showing the year in school of males vs. females.

 

5.   Make an X-Y scatter plot (with trend line) showing the height of students vs. the age of students.

 

6.   Make an X-Y scatter plot (with trend line) comparing the year in school vs. eye color (you will have to give each color a number).

 

7.   Make a line graph showing the average age of students for each year in school.

 

8.   Make an X-Y scatter plot (with trend line) showing birth month vs. height (you will have to convert birth month to a number).

 

9.   Compare your graphs to other students.  Write down whether each graph seems to match those of other students or not.  Do the graphs with more data tend to match those of other students better than graphs with less data?

 

10. What can you say from looking at graphs #6 and #8?  Do these graphs "prove" anything?  Can you use graph #6 to accurately predict the eye color of students based on their year in school?  Can you use graph #8 to accurately predict a student's height based on his birth month?  Why or why not?

 

11. For each of these graphs, predict whether or not you think they would be the same if you interviewed every student in the school.  Why or why not? 

 

12. If you wanted to interview a small number of students (20-30) and use that data to make generalizations about the whole school, how would you pick the students to interview?  Why?

 

Statistics:

 

1.   Is there a real difference between the average age of the girls and the average age of the boys you interviewed?  Use a t-test to determine.

 

2.   What is the correlation between height and gender?  How strong is this correlation?  Use a linear regression.

 

3.   Is there a real difference between the average year in school of the girls and the average year in school of the boys you interviewed?

 

4.   What is the correlation between birth month and year in school?  How strong is this correlation?  Even if this correlation is very strong, is it meaningful?  Why or why not?

 

5.   Does a linear regression tell you if you’ve collected enough data to be certain of a trend?  Explain.

 

6.   Compare the average age of all students in the class that you found to the average age of two other groups.  Are the averages different?  Are there real (statistical) difference sbetween these averages?  Use t-tests to determine.

 

7.   Compare the average year in school of all students that you interviewed to the average year in school of two other groups.  Are the averages different?  Are there real (statistical) differences between these averages?  Use t-tests to determine.

 

8.   Compare the average eye color of all students that you interviewed to the average eye color of two other groups (you and the other groups will have to convert eye color to a number first, and you’ll all have to use the same numbers!)  Are the averages different?  Are there real (statistical) differences between these averages?  Use t-tests to determine.
