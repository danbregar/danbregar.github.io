“Answer Key” – Vegetation of the Willamette Valley Study Guide

Field Biology

 

1.   The Native Americas burned the prairies to create a prairie-open woodlands complex, most likely to improve hunting (for example: deer, grasshoppers, bee honey) and the production of useful plants (for example: tarweed, sunflower).

 

2.   Settlers since the mid-1800’s have modified the landscape with grazing and agricultural practices, modified burning (no longer to manage game and wild plants; instead, the settlers burned to remove grain stubble or plant debris), logging, and residential/industrial development.

 

3.   The fir trees are very fire-resistant; thus, they were generally burned off by the Native American fires.  After the fires were stopped by settlers, the young fir trees were able to grow.  Since they grow very tall very quickly, the firs are able to overtop broadleaf trees.  Deprived of sunlight, the broadleaf trees die back, leaving a forest dominated by firs. 

 

4.   The oak trees, particularly thick-barked older trees, are fire tolerant.  In addition, frequent burnings left the landscape relatively low in fuel; thus the fires were cool and less likely to kill the fire tolerant oaks.

 

5.   Shantz and Zon meant that if there were no human management (i.e., agriculture, fire, development, logging, etc.) that Douglas Fir would likely dominate the Willamette Valley in the areas now occupied by prairies and oak woodlands.  This is due to the process described in #3.  Apparently, the frequency of natural fires is not great enough and it is not dry enough to discourage the growth of fir trees.
