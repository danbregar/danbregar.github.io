Example Literature Review

Field Biology

 

Author:  Mark V. Wilson and Deborah Clark

Title:  Controlling invasive Arrhenatherum elatius and promoting native prairie grasses through mowing

Source:  http://oregonstate.edu/~wilsomar/Papers.htm

 

Review:

Purpose/Question – The study was designed to investigate the effect of mowing on native and non-native grasses.  The frequency and intensity of mowing were both tested.  The data collected focused primarily on vegetative cover and flowers for A. elatius and D. californica.

 

Hypotheses – There were six hypotheses in this paper, all clearly stated and delineated.

1.   Mowing would be more effective during flowering

2.   Multiple mowings per year would be more effective

3.   Vegetative mowing, in addition to flower mowing, would be more effective

4.   As non-native grasses decrease, native grasses would increase seedling production

5.   Mowing would have less effect on grasses with shorter foliage

6.   Removing A. elatius would allow native grasses to increase in abundance

 

Research Methods, Materials, and Experimental Design – The study was conducted at a Wildlife Refuge near Salem, Oregon.  Four blocks of eight treatments were established and maintained over five years.  Each treatment varied the frequency, timing, and intensity of mowing.  The critical data collected was plant cover, germination, number of inflorescences, and litter depth.  The study seems to be very well-designed and geared towards a statistical analysis.  It is apparent that the authors took considerable care in making sure their study was thorough enough to be statistically significant.

 

Results/Data – An exhaustive statistical analysis demonstrated a decrease in A. elatius and an increase in D. californica in all treatments when compared to the control treatment.  The results were illustrated with several graphs and charts that compared experimental plots with control plots.

 

Discussion/Conclusions –The most effective treatment was mowing at 10-15 cm, twice per spring, leaving cut litter in the plot.  In general, all of the authors’ hypotheses were supported, except hypothesis 4.  However, one side effect of mowing was that it increased the germination of annual, non-native grasses.  Therefore, the authors recommended mowing once per year and removing litter – the treatment that had the greatest overall impact on the native / non-native plant ratio.
