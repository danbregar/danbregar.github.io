Introduction

 

Question:

            The objective of this study is to determine the relationship between the amount of rainfall and the growth of trailing blackberry.  The goal is to place trailing blackberry in different locations around the Noyes property.  The locations will include an open area and areas with various amounts of trees to serve as “rain blockers.”  Then we will measure the amount of rainfall in each area and observe the growth of the trailing blackberry. 

 

Background Research:

            Trailing blackberry has a significant effect on the environment.  There are a number of advantages and disadvantages of Trailing blackberry.  One advantage of Trailing blackberry includes the use of its berries.  Humans eat the berries and often use them in pies, jams, etc.  However, more often it is the berries from Himalayan blackberry that are desired by humans for consumption.  Browsing animals such as deer and goats depend on blackberry as a major food source.  Birds also eat the berries.  The leaves can be used for medical treatments such as for chest ailments and astringents.  Also, the blackberry flowers provide pollen for honeybees (“Blackberry” 2000).

One disadvantage is that it is an invasive species.  It spreads across the floor thereby blocking sunlight, soil, and space from other plants.  Not only does it take away habitat from other plants, but it also takes away habitat from larger animals.  At the same time as removing living space from large animals, it also provides a shelter for smaller animals like birds, rabbits, foxes, and other vermin.  Some of these animals can be considered pests.  Another inconvenience of Trailing blackberry is its thorns.  This affects humans and grazing animals.  The last major effect on the environment is that Trailing blackberry can be a serious fire hazard (“Blackberry” 2000).

Plants are made up of 70% water and require it for several reasons.  During the beginning stages, the plant seed needs water to soften the seed coat and allow the root and leaf inside to push it open.  During its life the plant needs water because water in the soil has dissolved minerals.  The plant absorbs this water through its roots and transports it to the stem and leaves thus taking in the nutrients that it needs to grow (Cook 2002). 

Different plants desire different amounts of water.  Some plants are drought tolerant by nature and can grow fine with low amounts of moisture.  Other plants, often big leaf plants, require more water.  When a plant has the adequate amount of water that it needs or more, it will focus on its top growth rather than developing a complex root system.  Plants that are used to dryer areas will develop larger root systems in order to find water deeper within the soil (Hadden 2005).  Trailing blackberry is an example of a plant that has an extensive root system and it is able to send out roots from the tips of the canes (“Rubus fruticosus aggregate” 2002). 

 

Site Location:

(December, 2005)

            The Noyes property is relatively flat in that it doesn’t have any major hills or swales.  There are a few places where there are tiny ponds/puddles.  These places are the only ones that have standing water and everywhere else just has moist soil.  The most abundant plants include a variety of trees, blackberry, and grasses.  There are signs of animals such as birds, gofers, and rabbits, but animals are rarely seen in the area.  Human interaction in this area includes multiple trails and occasionally some garbage is found.

 

Hypothesis:

            We expect that Trailing blackberry grows well in areas with less rainfall.  This is because it has an extensive root system, which can penetrate deep into the soil (“Rubus fruticosus aggregate” 2002).  Plants that have large root spreads generally live in dryer places (Hadden 2005).  In wet areas large root systems would not be required because the plants wouldn’t need to search for water deep within the soil.  The water would all be at the top of the soil and the blackberry would only need shallow roots.
