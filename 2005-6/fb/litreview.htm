Literature Reviews

Field Biology

 

A scientific literature review is a critical analysis of the research conducted on a particular topic or question in the field of science.

 

Conducting scientific literature reviews help you design your own scientific study by requiring you to: 

1.   Determine the research and methods previously conducted in the field.

2.   Discover what remains to be learned in the field.

3.   Highlight mistakes, difficulties, or ethical issues encountered by others in order to save time and money with your own study.

4.   Identify parties with vested interested in this area of study including research competitors and potential partners.

5.   Clearly define parameters for your own scientific study.

 

Steps you will take in creating your scientific literature reviews:

1.   Identify current work from pertinent publications.

2.   Summarize and record the contents of each for your records.

 

Scientific Sources: 

Peer reviewed journal articles are the best sources.  These are scientific journals that will not publish a scientific study until fellow scientists have scrutinized it.  Peer reviewed journal articles are considered more scientifically valid than other sources of information. 

                        Examples:  Science, Nature, Molecular Ecology

                        Non-examples:  National Geographic, Discover, Field and Stream

 

Primary sources are first-hand accounts of events, such as diaries, patient charts, letters, eyewitness accounts, professional (peer reviewed) journals, autobiographies, research reports, and information collected by interviews and questionnaires. 

 

Secondary sources are second-hand accounts. These include: histories, biographies, textbooks, and materials that give secondary analysis of the data found in primary records. Do not rely solely on secondary sources.  If you find information in a secondary source, try to obtain the primary source from which the information was initially obtained.  This allows you to check for errors.

 

Community professionals in the field of interest are also good sources of information and referrals for relevant professional publications and information.

 

 

 

Include a summary and analysis of the following in your article reviews:  (Note that these sections may or may not be clearly labeled in the article you are reading  sometimes, you have to dig a little bit to discover these items)

 

Author:  Who are the researchers?

 

Title:  What is the title of this article?

 

Source:  What was the journal or website where this article was found?  If this is a website, make sure that you have typed it correctly  you should be able to cut and paste this information into a web browser and find the exact paper you used.

 

Purpose / Question:  What are the researchers investigating?

 

Hypothesis:  What does the researcher expect to see?

 

Research Methods, Materials and Experimental Design:  What are the researchers planning to do?  Where will they be doing their research?  What unique equipment or materials did they need?  What are their sampling methods?

 

Data / Results:  What information did the researchers find?  In the paper, this will likely be summarized in data tables, charts, and graphs, with additional information in the text.

 

Discussion / Conclusions:  What are the implications of this study?  Was the hypothesis determined to be correct or incorrect?  What specific recommendations for further research are given?
