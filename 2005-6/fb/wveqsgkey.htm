“Answer” Key – Willamette Valley

Ecology Quiz Study Guide

Field Biology

 

1.      The Native Americans in our area managed their environment extensively through burning and planting regimes.  They burned the area in order to maintain the open prairies and Garry Oak communities – they preferred this because it helped them move through the area, it helped them find and catch game, and it created open spaces that they could plant desirable plant species such as Camas and Tarweed.  They also had large areas of land (typically wet prairies) where they maintained fields of these plants (typically, these areas were used year after year, particularly since Camas takes several years to grow to edible size).

 

2.      The Willamette River has been dammed in several places on its main stem and on tributaries.  It has also been constrained into a single channel along most of its length.  Cottonwoods require exposed, fresh soil and/or gravel in order to germinate (sprout) – open soil provides the cottonwoods with plenty of light, nutrients, water, and space to grow.  Before floods were controlled by dams, there were frequent flooding events along the Willamette River.  These floods removed existing vegetation and soil and exposed fresh gravel along the river.

 

3.      Channel constraints prevent the river channel from moving.  In the past, the Willamette River channel was not always in the same place from year to year – erosion caused one side of the river to be eaten away, and fresh soil and gravel was exposed on the other side of the river.  This exposed soil was perfect for cottonwoods to grow (there’s more information above in the answer to #2).

 

4.      Unlike the Native Americans, we currently do not burn large areas of native prairie in the Willamette Valley.  This allows shrub and tree species to exist in areas where they formerly would have been burned off yearly.  As a result, there are many areas of native prairie and Garry oak communities that are slowly becoming forested.  This happens because without the yearly burns, young tree seedlings (both oaks and firs) survive and grow.  However, the fir trees grow faster than the oak trees.  So eventually, areas that were once covered with prairie or even oak trees are eventually covered with fir trees.  Since the oak trees require lots of sunlight to live, once they have been overgrown by the fir trees, the oaks eventually die.

 

5.      The Willamette Valley was largely open prairie and oak woodlands.  Prairies consisted of grasses, flowers, and occasional small shrubs.  Oak woodlands were generally relatively open – there might be a few trees clumped together, but it generally did not look like a forest – instead, it was just a small group of trees surrounded by prairie.  These prairies and woodlands were maintained by fire (see the answer to #4 above).

 

6.      Much of the land was a wetland in 1850.  During the summer months, there may have only been a few channels of water draining through the valley.  During the wet season, though, much of the valley floor was likely covered in water - small creeks, ponds, and standing water.  The vegetation of the entire valley would likely have consisted of plants that prefer to live in areas with lots of water – some examples that we’ve looked at in class are Oregon Ash, Red Alder, and Sitka Spruce trees along with, snowberry, cow parsnip, and red-osier dogwood.  The valley would have been relatively open, as it is likely that Native Americans burned the land fairly often to clear out many of the shrubs and small trees.  Now, the valley is mostly farmland.  It is not as wet as it used to be, since humans have deliberately drained much of the land.  The vegetation along Jackson and Frazier Creeks still resembles that of the former wetland areas, although non-native species such as Himalayan Blackberry, teasel, and wild carrot are now common.
