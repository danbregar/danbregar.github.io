Visual Stream Assessment (Water Quality)

Field Biology

 

This is a modified version of a standard protocol designed to give researchers a way to quickly evaluate a body of water for possible water quality issues.  This method is not comprehensive, nor is it a substitute for a detailed chemical and biological analysis; however, it often will point researchers in a certain direction when they are measuring water quality.

 

1.   Point Source Pollution.  Do you see any evidence of areas where pollutants may be entering the body of water directly?  Look for pipes, culverts, drainage ditches, or other man-made objects.

 

2.   Non-Point Source Pollution.  Based on the surrounding land use, do you anticipate that there might be pollutants entering the water from a large area of land?  Think about fertilizer, herbicide, and insecticide applications.

 

3.   Buffer Vegetation.  Is there a strip of vegetation along the perimeter of the body of water?  If so, how wide is it?  Does it consist of native, non-native, or mixed vegetation?

 

4.   Visual Evaluation of Water.  What is the appearance of the water?  Is it discolored, are there oily patches, or is there a large amount of foam?  How turbid does the water look?

 

5.   Physical Evaluation of the Water.  What would you estimate the water temperature to be?  Does the water have an odor?

 

6.   Erosion.  Does the bank appear stable?  Do you see rip-rap or other stabilizing features?  If you are evaluating a stream, is it sinuous (winding) or straight?

 

7.   Miscellaneous.  Are there other observations worth noting?  Consider historical issues, land-use issues, or anecdotal evidence you may have.
