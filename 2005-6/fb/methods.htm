II. Methods:

Your methods section should clearly explain how you will undertake your study.  Your goal should be to describe all of the relevant details of your study in such a way that someone else could duplicate your study exactly in order to check to see if your data is correct.  Mention the materials you used only when they are not household terms, there was more than one material you could have used, or someone would need to use your exact materials in order to duplicate your results.  For example, you do not need to say that you used a thermometer to measure water temperature.  However, you do need to say that you kept your crawdad in an aerated aquarium or that you placed your trapped mice in a 2’ by 2’ cardboard box.

In a step-by-step format, clearly describe the methods you will use to take data.  Be as specific as you can; however, you may need to change these methods as your study progresses.  Here is an example of PART of an acceptable methods section:

“1.  I will plant equal densities (5 plants in a 0.25 m2 plot) of ivy and blackberry shoots in sandy soil to limit the amount of nutrients and water.  There will be 10 replications of this treatment (Smith 2001). 

2.  I will provide each plot with 100mL of water mixed with 5 drops of MiracleGro plant food each day (Anderson 1988). 

3.  Then, I will measure the plant growth for each individual as follows:  total increase in shoot length, # of new leaves, # of new shoots, # of flowers, and % cover (Behove 1991); data will be taken daily for 4 weeks.”

Whenever possible, you should use established scientific methods to take your measurements.  This will ensure that a) your measurements are taken correctly and b) you can compare your results to the results of other scientists.  Therefore, all of your methods should be cited.  If you have a sentence or paragraph in your methods section that is not cited, it will be ignored unless you have discussed it with your instructor prior to turning in your work.

In general, scientists do not need to fully describe procedures they will be using if the procedures are generally known, used, and accepted.  For example, a researcher might state that he or she is using the "line transect" method of surveying large mammals.  However, since your study is aimed at a very different audience than most scientific studies, you do not have the luxury of assuming your readers will recognize your methods.  In other words, you need to clearly spell out all your methods - in the example above, you would need to describe in detail what “increase in shoot length” means.

When completed, your methods section should address the following questions:

1.  What are the specific, detailed techniques you will use to collect your data?  What materials will you need (do not list common, everyday equipment such as string or rulers)?  How will you set up your sites or plots?

2.  Where will your sites be located (use GPS coordinates if possible in addition to a verbal description)?  Make sure you have the location of control and experimental sites, if applicable.

3.  Where, within your larger sites, will you actually collect data?  How big will these plots be?  How many plots will there be?  How will you choose where to locate your plots?  How many times will you need to collect data?  How many total samples will you need to collect?

4.  How will you need to store and/or prepare your samples after they have been collected?

5.  How many replicates will you need?  If you are investigating cause and effect, how many control or experimental setups will you need?

6.  After you have collected your data, what equations will you use to summarize or analyze this data?  Include formulas if they are not commonly known (for example, you do not need to include a formula for finding an average, but you would need to include a formula for calculating the percentage water content of soil).

Your methods section should also include a blank data sheet that you can print out and bring to the field with you in order to collect your data and should include a map, diagram, or aerial photo that indicates the location of your data collection sites.

 

References

Your methods should include a COMPLETE, properly formatted references section using APA format (see the CVHS library web site at http://www2.corvallis.k12.or.us/cvhs/staff/westlaj/index4.htm for details).  All cited references – whether you used a direct quotation or not – should be included.

Note - In-Text Citation Format

If you know the author of the web site, your citaton should look like this:

(Bregar 2003)

If you don’t know the author, use the title in quotation marks (just use a little bit with an ellipsis if it’s long):

(“Arsenic and environmental…” 2001)

If you don’t know the year, leave it off:

(“Health effects of…”)

Here is an example of how an in-text citation would be used in a paper:

Arsenic is sometimes found in soils that developed through volcanic activity (“Arsenic and environmental” 2001).

In-text citations are required in your research, but they do not replace a full list of references – each paragraph should be followed by a list of references in proper format as shown on the CVHS library web site.
