Writing Tips

 

Follow the Format!

If you follow the required format, address all the questions, include all necessary information, and cite your sources, your science grade (F, D, C, and A) will be quite high.  Read through the format a couple of times to familiarize yourself with it; then refer back to it frequently while writing.

Comma Splices

Do not use comma splices - two sentences that are combined with a comma.

INCORRECT:  “A carcinogen can cause forms of cancers and tumors in human and laboratory animals, a developmental toxin can severely harm an infant or child.”

To correct, use a semi-colon or add a conjunction:

CORRECT:  “…human and laboratory animals, and a developmental…” – or –

CORRECT:  “…human and laboratory animals; a developmental…”

Semicolons

Semicolons can be used to combine two complete sentences:

CORRECT:  “Arsenic is a highly toxic element; it occurs naturally in soil, water, and air.”

“You”

Don’t use this pronoun!  Use I or we instead.

INCORRECT:  “…the further down you go the more arsenic you will find…”

CORRECT:  “…the further down we go the more arsenic we will find…”

Transition Words

Use transition words to tie sentences or paragraphs together – they alert the read as to how the previous information will help them understand subsequent information.  Some good transition words to use are “for example”, “although”, “however”, “unlike ____”, “in addition to”, “separate from”, and “similarly”.  There are many other good transition words you can use.

CORRECT:  “Arsenic is highly toxic to most mammals.  For example, arsenic in drinking water can lead to cancer in humans.”

CORRECT:  “In most forms, arsenic presents a severe health hazard.  However, in its elemental form, it is relatively safe.”

Big Words

Big words are fine to use if you know what they mean.  If you don’t, it will likely be obvious:

INCORRECT:  “Arsenic is harmful to human health.  Arsenic can be found in many sources.  Arsenic can be in water, soil, or living organisms.  Arsenic can move around in the environment through the processes of dissolution, adsorption, and ionic exchange.”

Conversational Tone

Try to avoid using words, phrases, or sentences that are similar to those you might use when you are talking. 

INCORRECT:  “…causing a major ecological problem.”

CORRECT:  “…causing ecological damage.”

INCORRECT:  “Our group of four kids from Crescent Valley High School…”

CORRECT:  “Our group of students from Crescent Valley High School…”

Effect/affect

In general, “effect” is a noun and “affect” is a verb.

CORRECT:  “The effect of the arsenic was deadly.”

CORRECT:  “Arsenic can affect many different organisms.”

It’s/its

“It’s” is a contraction that means “it is”.  “Its” is a possessive like “his”, “hers”, or “ours”.

CORRECT:  “Arsenic can cause many health problems if it’s in drinking water.”

CORRECT:  “Ames Creek may have many different chemical forms of arsenic in its water.”

Note – it is generally considered bad form to use contractions in a scientific paper.

Quotes

Quotes should be short and necessary.  If you can retain the meaning of a sentence by paraphrasing it, do so.  Quotes are generally necessary only when the original author’s style, tone, or particular wording are essential to the point you are making.

Complete Sentences

All sentences should have a subject (not just a noun) and a verb.

INCORRECT:  “Then if the amount of arsenic in the plants affect the deer that eats those plants.”

CORRECT:  “Then we will determine if the amount of arsenic in the plants affect the deer that eats those plants.”

Sentence Fluency

Here are some tips for increasing your sentence fluency:

1.  Vary the length and structure of your sentences.

“Arsenic forms odorless, tasteless compounds.  When arsenic is present in drinking water, it can cause health problems such as digestive disorders, skin lesions, cancer, or even death.”

2.  Use transition words.

“Arsenic is rarely present in high concentrations in water or soil.  However, almost every soil or water sample contains some arsenic.”

3.  Use semicolons or commas + conjunctions to combine sentences.

“Several studies have been conducted regarding arsenic in drinking water; these studies have been inconclusive.  Many scientists are finding that the chemical form of arsenic is critical to its effects, but it is difficult to determine the chemical form of arsenic using in-field tests.”

Proofread

It is a good idea to have someone else read through your paper to find mistakes that you are “automatically” correcting.

INCORRECT:  “How does arsenic plants?”

INCORRECT:  “We will find weather arsenic is in the soil or not.”

Citation Format

If you know the author it should look like this:

(Bregar 2003)

If you don’t know the author, use the title in quotation marks (just use a little bit with an ellipsis if it’s long):

(“Arsenic and environmental…” 2001)

If you don’t know the year, leave it off:

(“Health effects of…”)

References

Use APA format (available on my web site); references should be in alphabetical order.  Look at a scientific paper to see what a good list of references looks like!

Organization

Use an outline to organize your thoughts, paragraphs, and sentences.  Here is an example of an outline that might be used for an introduction section:

I.   Introduction

A. Question – WITRB soil arsenic and plant growth?

B. Background info

1.  Arsenic

a.  What is it?

b.  Where is it?

c.  Health effects

2.  Soil

a.  Definitions and terms

b.  Why is it ecologically important?

3.  Plants

C. Site location

1.  Sweet Home

2.  Map

3.  Ames Creek

4.  Specific sites

D. Hypothesis (cite source!)
