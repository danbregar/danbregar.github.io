Final Presentation Guidelines

Advanced Field Biology

 

Here is the format for your final presentation.  The final presentations will be held on the evening of Wednesday, May 25 in room ALS 4000 on OSU’s campus.  Each person should speak between 4-6 minutes; when your group reaches its limit, I will cut you off (as nicely as possible).  A schedule will be posted on Friday, May 20th.  Each group member should contribute equally during the presentations.  The audience will be composed of students, parents, school administrators, mentors, and interested community members.

 

On Wednesday, May 18 and Thursday, May 19, initial run-throughs will be held.  Those groups who have not achieved satisfactory progress on their presentations will not be presenting on May 25; instead, these groups will present on a later date with a 30% penalty.

 

Your grade will be compiled from evaluations by Mr. Bregar and the rest of the class.

 

Requirements:

1.   Must be created in PowerPoint (one presentation per group)

2.   Include all of the relevant information from your study, including:

a.   Introduction (2 – 3 slides total for your question, your background information, your hypothesis, and your site location)

b.   Methods (1 - 2 slides, should be in past tense)

c.   Results (1 - 2 slides total for your raw data and calculated numbers – include hypothetical data if appropriate)

d.   Analysis (2 - 3 slides total for your statistical analysis, conclusions, generalizations, and implications of your study – should be in past tense where appropriate)

e.   Acknowledgements (1 slide) - thank everyone who provided you with help

3.   Should be a minimum of 4 minutes and a maximum of 6 minutes per person

4.   Each group member should be prepared for detailed questions from audience members

 

Important Notes:

  • In order to make sure that the computer equipment is loaded and ready to go with everyone’s presentation, you MUST give me a copy of your presentation by 4:00 pm on Tuesday, May 24th.  You can save your presentation on a CD-ROM, a floppy disk, or a zip disk in Microsoft Windows format, or you can save your presentation to your H drive and then copy it to the HP computer in F-18.  If I do not have access to the final copy of your presentation by 4:00 pm on May 24th, you will get not present on the 25th and you will be penalized 30%.  Please plan ahead so your presentation is ready on time!

 

  • If you do not have enough data for a full analysis, you should create hypothetical data.  All hypothetical data should be clearly marked as such, and it should be analyzed in a “what if…” manner – in other words, you will be describing ONE possible answer to your question, but not necessarily the only possible answer.  This should be done in such a way that there is no confusion over which data is real and which data is hypothetical.

 

  • If your study will require additional data collection (either this year or in the future), make sure your presentation clearly indicates what data needs to be collected, and when and how this will take place.

 

  • I have a digital camera you can use to take pictures of your sites and your project.  This is a great way to improve the visual appeal of your presentation without distracting from the overall content.

 

  • I will be arranging informal presentation practices during the week of May 167th; this will give you the opportunity to get feedback from me and from your classmates.  If there is time, groups may have the chance to give their presentation more than once.

 

  • You may use notecards and outlines to help with your presentation.  However, each group member should have memorized at the very least the following information:

 

    • What is the question your group was trying to answer?
    • From a scientific point of view, why was this study important?
    • What were the methods you used to answer your question?
    • What did your data show?
