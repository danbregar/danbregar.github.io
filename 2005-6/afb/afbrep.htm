Final Report Guidelines

Advanced Field Biology

 

Here is the format for your final report.  Each group needs to submit only one final report (not one per person).  Please include the headings for each section in your paper and make sure that the headings are in the order indicated below.  The due date for your rough draft is Thursday, June 2.  The final draft is due Friday, June 10. I reserve the right to ask for interim revisions depending on the quality of your rough draft.  Please make sure to incorporate ALL of the changes I requested when you submitted the final draft of your proposal.

 

Seniors – since you have a shorter school year than other students, I am happy to accept and review your work early.  However, if I do not have your completed work by Friday, June 10, you will receive a G or an F in the class (depending on the status of your other work).

 

Abstract:

This should be a summary of all of the sections below.  It should be one paragraph of around 200-250 words.

 

Introduction:

This should include a revised version of the question/purpose, background information, and hypothesis you stated in your proposal that reflects any changes you have made as your project has progressed.  Include citations where appropriate.  This section should be in past tense when it refers to things that you have now completed.

 

Methods:

This should be a detailed, step-by-step list of your methodology.  This should be completely updated from your proposal and include any additions or modifications you made to your proposed methods.  Include citations where appropriate.  This section should be in past tense when it refers to things that you have now completed.

 

Results:

This section should include a verbal and numeric summary of your data.  Numerical data should be entered into a spreadsheet as a table.  You can copy the data into a word processor for your final report; however, it is acceptable to simply print a copy of the spreadsheet as your data.  The printed version should be neat, easily read, and clearly labeled and formatted.  Where appropriate, calculate average values for your measurements.  Where useful, create a graph or chart for your data.  Since each group is collecting a different type of data, you should check with your instructor frequently to make sure that your data table is in an acceptable format.

 

Your data should take the form of both RAW data (the original measurements and calculations you wrote down on your data sheets) and COMPILED data (smaller spreadsheets that show only average values and other calculations).

 

Along with your data tables, this section should include a detailed description of your results.  Include a narrative of your activities (on a day to day basis, if necessary) along with periodical observations.

 

Discussion:

This section is the most important part of your report.  You should draw conclusions and generalizations from your study, point out any errors or exceptions that you have observed, and discuss any practical and theoretical implications of your work.  In general, any experiments you have done will not prove anything; they will simply provide evidence for a position.  As such, all of your statements should be backed by statistical evidence wherever possible.  For example, instead of saying “…our results prove that non-native plants require more nutrients than native plants”, write “…our results indicate that non-native plants require more nutrients than native plants (p=0.03).”  I will be happy to review your discussion on a daily basis if that would be helpful.

 

In your discussion, make sure that you address the following points:

a.   What, according to your data, was the answer to your question?  What does your statistical test tell you about your data?  In other words, was your hypothesis correct?

b.   Why did you get the results you did?  Your goal is to explain the scientific causes that might generate the results you saw.  You cannot be certain about the reasons you propose in answer to this question; instead, each reason you suggest might be the basis for a future scientific experiment.  You should have between 3-5 possible reasons that explain why you obtained the results you did.  For example, if you find that there is a correlation between the elevation of a plant and the amount of water it needs to survive, you should speculate as to WHY you see this relationship.  Maybe it is because at higher elevations, temperatures are cooler, so plants need to respire more to generate heat.  Maybe it is because at higher elevations, it rains more, so plants have become adapted to higher amounts of water.  These are ideas could be tested experimentally; these are the sorts of ideas you should include in your discussion.

c.   What could you and your group have done differently to improve your project?  In other words, what advice would you give to future groups if they wanted to duplicate or extend your study?

d.   What are the next logical steps for your study?  In other words, if you had another semester to continue working on your project, what types of studies or experiments would you set up?  How would these studies or experiments help you to understand the relationship between the factors you are studying?

 

References:

Include a list of references in standard format.

 

Data Sheets:

Please attach all of the daily data you collected during the course of your project.  Attach the actual data sheets (or photocopies of the actual data sheets), not recopied data or data entered into a computer.
