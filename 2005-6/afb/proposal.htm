Project Proposal Guidelines

Advanced Field Biology

 

Your project proposal will communicate to me exactly what your group will be studying, what materials your group will need, and when your group will be performing each task.  In addition, your proposal needs to be developed through research; in other words, you will not be making decisions based on your opinions, you will be making decisions based on the opinions of other researchers who have done similar studies to yours.  This will ensure that 1) your methodology is accepted by the scientific community and 2) the data you collect will properly support your study goals.  Each group needs to submit only ONE proposal.  This document should be in future tense, and remember to cite all of your sources in the proper format.

 

You should have decided on your group members and chosen a rough topic by Wednesday, February 23.  You should have your question determined by Thursday, February 24 (in the following format:  “What is the relationship between ________ of __________ and _________ of _________?”; at that point, you should begin your research.  Your introduction should be complete by Friday, March 4.  The first draft of your completed proposal is due on Friday, March 18, and should include at least 15 references (five of which should be from scientific papers; the other ten should be from acceptable, scientific web sites).  Please note that this proposal should be completely finished to the best of your ability.  I will return graded proposals to you on Tuesday, March 29; your final draft is due on Tuesday, April 5.

 

Proposal Format

I.    Introduction

In this section, state in one or two paragraphs what your research question is.  The first sentence should be a brief, concise description of your question (for example, "The goal of this study is to determine the distribution of native and non-native shrubs along Jackson Creek on the Crescent Valley campus").  This should be followed with supporting sentences that add details (for example, "Our goals are to map out the existing distributions of native and non-native shrubs; we also intend to classify groups of shrubs as primarily native or primarily non-native.  Our data will be entered into a GIS database that will allow further studies to monitor changes in shrub distributions over time").

 

This section should also include background information.  Write this section from a scientific point of view - what is the scientific importance of the topics you are covering and what is the scientific relevance of the data you expect to collect?  Think about the following questions when writing your background information:

1.  What is the ecological significance of each of the factors you are studying?  For example, why are non-native plants ecologically significant?  What do native plants contribute to the ecology of an area?  What role does soil moisture play in plant growth?  (CITE SOURCES!)

2.  What evidence did you find in your research that makes you think that there might be an interaction or relationship between your factors?  (CITE SOURCES)

3.  From your research, describe the importance of your question from a scientific perspective.  Why is this a topic worth studying?

Your background information should include citations, be properly formatted, come from your research and be 3-5 pages long.

 

This section should include a detailed site description and site location.  Give as much information about your site as possible, including types of plants present, human interactions and development, drawings, and diagrams.  Your site location should allow anyone to locate your site (even someone who is not familiar with the Corvallis area) and should include a map (printed or carefully hand-drawn).  Your site description and location should be 1-2 pages long.

 

Finally, this section should include a hypothesis (unless your instructor waives this requirement).  Your hypothesis should explain what you expect to see and why; for example, "…we expect that non-native shrubs will dominate along Jackson Creek due to human influences such as disturbance and channelization.  In addition, non-native shrubs are generally known to outcompete most native shrubs in the Willamette Valley (Philips, 1997)".  Do NOT try to develop your hypothesis before you have done your research.  Instead, you should develop your hypothesis in the process of doing your research.  In other words, use the information you uncover in your research to help you figure out what the likely answer to your question is!  Remember that all of your statements should be backed by citations from your research.

 

II.  Methods

Your methods should clearly explain what you are going to do to carry out your study.  Wherever possible, cite the sources of your methods.  This section should be written in paragraph form or, where appropriate, in a list form, using complete sentences.  Depending on your study, your methods section should be anywhere from 1-5 pages long.

 

Start by describing your initial project set-up.  How many sites or plots will you have?  How many replicates will you need?  How will you locate and randomize (if necessary) your sites?  Where, specifically, will your sites be located within the larger area of your project (for example, if your project will be carried out along Jackson Creek, identify specific locations along the creek where your data collection will occur).  How large will your sites be and how will you mark and identify them?

 

In general, scientists do not need to fully describe procedures they will be using if the procedures are generally known, used, and accepted.  For example, a researcher might state that he or she is using the "line transect" method of surveying large mammals.  However, since your study is aimed at a very different audience than most scientific studies, you do not have the luxury of assuming your readers will recognize your methods.  In other words, you need to clearly spell out all your methods - in the example above, you would need to describe in detail what the line transect method means.

 

A critical part of your methods should be to describe how you intend to analyze your data.  What statistical tests will you use?  How will you determine whether your hypothesis is correct or not?  To answer these questions, you will probably need to supplement your readings with information supplied to you by a teacher or mentor.

 

Due to the limitations of conducting research in a high school, it is critical that this section includes a list of materials that clearly indicates who will be providing them.  Do not assume your instructor will provide a certain material; ask first.  This section can be "short and sweet" - make a table of materials and list who will provide each one.

 

III. Timeline

In this section, you should spell out each task of your project set up, data collection and analysis procedures (you do not need to include research activities, writing your proposal, or preparing your final report).  This does not need to be supported by research; your timeline is an estimate of when you will be doing the various tasks that comprise your project.  Please type your timeline as a table so it is easy to read and follow.

 

IV. Data Collection Form

Your data should be collected onto a form that you design and photocopy.  As you conduct your research, your data collection form may change in response to discoveries you make along the way.  This is fine; the importance of including a copy of your form in your proposal is to ensure that you give careful thought to your data before you begin collecting information.

 

Although you do not need to cite references on your data collection form, the design of your form will be strongly influenced by your research.  For example, when reading a previous study, you may notice that the researchers recorded the date, time, and weather conditions.  If you include spaces for this information on your data collection form, you will remember to record it every time you sample.

 

V.  References
