OBJECTIVE:     To learn how to utilize spreadsheet functions to perform calculations and display results in graphical form.

 

DIRECTIONS:

1.  Open the excel spreadsheet file entitled “Motion Mysteries Spread Sheet Template”.

 

2.  Save this spreadsheet wherever you can (i.e. your H drive or the shared folder we have had you saving things too lately).  Be sure to give it a unique name.  WRITE THE NAME OF THIS FILE DOWN IN YOUR JOURNAL!

 

3.  One advantage of using spreadsheets is that they can do lots of calculations relatively quickly.  To illustrate this, the spreadsheet in front of you has data for 10 hypothetical runners.  Ultimately, we want to create a v-vs-t graph for these runners.  The first thing we want to do is repeat the calculations you performed on Friday.  We want to duplicate the table you made Friday in class.  Below the table, copy cells A1:A11.  Paste them in Cells A13:A23 below the table.

 

4.  Next, create the following column headings for our new tables.  Starting in Cell B13, enter the following headings from left to right:  “0-5m time”, “5-10m time”, “10-15m time”, “15-20m time”, “Velocity at Start”, “0-5m velocity”, “5-10m velocity”, “10-15m velocity”,  “15-20m velocity”.  The last heading should be in Cell J13.

 

5.  Center all of these headings and make them bold.

 

6.  The time it took each runner to go the first five meters is simply the first time listed in the top table.  Copy Cells C2:C10 into Cells B14:B23.

 

7.  We can have Excel calculate for us the rest of the times for our new table.  For instance, to calculate the time it took Runner #1 to go from 5 to 10 meters, we should enter the following in Cell C14: “=D2-C2”.  Be sure to hit return when you have typed this.  The “=” tells Excel that this is a mathematical formula to calculate.  The return tells Excel that you are done entering the formula.  Also, instead of typing “D2” and “C2” in the equation, you can also use the cursor to insert the letter and number of a cell by clicking on the cell.  Take care here – if you click on the wrong cell or select extra cells, you will probably get an error message when you hit return.

 

8.  To finish the rest of the 5-10 meter times for the other nine runners, select Cell C14 and while holding your mouse key, drag down to highlight the rest of the 5-10 meter time cells.  Next, holding down the control key and the D key at the same time will “copy down” this formula.  The beauty of this tool is that doing it this way, Excel knows to use the data for Runner #2 for Runner #2’s time, the data for Runner #3 for Runner #3’s time, etc….

 

9.  See if you can repeat these steps by completing all of the rest of the times in your new table.

 

10.  Now we have to calculate velocities.  Enter zeros for all of the starting velocities.  Enter the following formula in Cell G14:  “=5/B14”.  Be sure to hit return when you are done.  Don’t forget the “=”.  And if you want, instead of typing “B14” you can use the curser to select this cell.

 

11.  Next, copy this formula down this column of your table for all of the rest of the runners using the COPY DOWN command as described above.

 

12.  Repeat Steps #10 and #11 for all of the remaining velocities in your new table.

 

13.  There are numerous ways in Excel to produce a chart.  Let’s produce a scatter plot chart depicting Runner #1’s velocity as a function of time.  Go up to the button that looks like a chart and click on it.  This will get you into the chart wizard.

 

14.  Select the “XY Scatter Plot” and click on “NEXT”.

 

15.  Click on the “Series” tab.  This is where we will tell Excel what cells of our spreadsheet we want plotted for each runner.

 

16.  Click on the “Add” button.

 

17.  Enter “Runner #1” in the name box.

 

18.  We want to select the cells that will serve as our X values (time goes on the x-axis).  Click on the little box at the right of the entry box that has a little red arrow.  Doing this will take you to your spreadsheet.  Select Cells B2:F2.  (Yes, you want to select zero at time zero as well!)  When done, click on the little box with the little red arrow on the “Source Data X-Values” dialog box.  This takes you back to the previous box.

 

19.  Repeat this procedure to select the Y-values.  This time, you will want to select Cells F14:J14.  These are the velocities for Runner #1 at the various time intervals.

 

20.  Click on the NEXT button.

 

21.  Enter “Runner’s Velocity vs. Time” for chart title, “Time (seconds)” for the X-axis label, and “Velocity (m/sec) for the Y-axis label.  Click on “NEXT”.

 

22.  Click “Finish” to insert your chart into your worksheet.

 

23.  Almost everything can be modified on your chart.  For instance, suppose you wish to connect your points with a line.  To do this, simply double click on one of the data points on your chart.  Under the “Patterns” tab, simply click the “Automatic” box for line.  If you want a smooth line, select this box as well.  Upon hitting okay, your data will be connected with a smooth line.  Now we are starting to create something you have already seen before – a velocity vs. time graph for Runner #1.

 

24.  The data for other runners can easily be added to your chart.  You may need to move your chart out of the way though first.

 

25.  First, select your chart.  You will know you have selected it when little black boxes appear around its edges.

 

26.  Then, from the Chart pull down menu, select the “Source Data” option.  The dialog box that appears now should be familiar to you.

 

27.  Click the “ADD” button.  Type in Runner #2 in the “Name” box.  Next, select the X-values (times) and the Y-values (velocities) as you did previously for Runner #1’s data.  BE SURE YOU SELECT RUNNER #2’s DATA!  But even if you make a mistake, another beauty of a spreadsheet is that it can always be changed later.

 

28.  Add data for all ten runners onto your chart.
