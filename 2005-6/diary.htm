Today started off as a relatively average day on the JR.  Mark pulled out all the stops – we look at basically the most significant data and conclusions that have come out of the drilling program:  cores that show catastrophic events (such as massive meteor strikes) and crazy/unexplainable climatic changes.  It was very cool – we had to synthesize much of the information we had looked at over the course of the previous nine days in order to really understand what was going on.

Day 9:  Monday, November 7, 2005

 

Well, we’re getting into Mexico now, and things are lookin’ good aboard the JR.  Today, we had our curriculum meeting outside.  Check out the picture of Leslie and the lovely Vanna aka J-Bob aka Jerry conducting the meeting.

 

 

Today we started with a cool live videoconference / lecture from Adam Klaus.  He told us about the remote sensing equipment that scientists have been stuffing down drill holes (for lack of a better term).  One of our original goals on this trip was to rework some of the seals on some of this equipment on a hole off of Vancouver Island.  However, the lousy weather forced us to abort that plan, so they’ll get to it in a couple of years.  I wish bad weather was a good enough excuse for me to put some things off for a couple of years.

 

Then, Lisa gave us some information on the seismic surveys they do aboard the ship and took us on a tour of the onboard geophysics lab.  It was so loud that we had to follow the bouncing ball in order to figure out what we were looking at.

Day 7:  Saturday, November 5, 2005

 

After sleeping in to a decadent 6:45, the group convened in the conference room to prepare for a tour of the ship, which was absolutely amazing.  Basically, the entire ship is designed for the sole purpose of drilling several thousand feet into the seafloor.  So the centerpiece of the ship is a gigantic derrick that’s used to raise and lower the drills and the pipes attached to the drills.  Here’s a picture so you can see how cool it is.

 

 

On the tour, we got to see all the equipment that the operators use to run the drills and the derrick.  We also toured the engine room, where several gigantic engines burn tons of fuel in order to propel and power the ship.  The whole affair is a combination of super high-tech stuff (like underwater cameras that allow the operators to drill into holes thousands of feet below sea level) and ridiculously low-tech stuff (like the mud they mix together to use as a lubricant and a cutting agent).

 

In the afternoon, it was curriculum, curriculum, curriculum.  We broke into several small groups in order to start creating and refining activities and groups of activities that we can teach related to geology and oceanography.  We also did a little show-and-tell where we explained our ideas to the rest of the groups, as you can see in the picture.

 

 

We took a time-out for the sunset (that’s Mary in the picture!) and dinner, then reconvened for picture sharing and some gift swapping.  I, of course, being a gigantic lame-o, did not bring any gifts to swap.  So instead, I gave the group the best gift of all:  I left.

 

 

Tomorrow, if the weather holds, which it will, since we’re now off the coast of Baja California, we’re going to have a BBQ dinner on the main deck of the ship.  What fun!

 

Day 6:  Friday, November 4, 2005

 

Today the weather was … beautiful.  Here’s a picture of the sunset last night (taken by Julie Marsteller, another teacher aboard the ship).

 

 

 

As you can see, the waves are way down and blue skies have emerged!  As a result, we were really able to get down to the nitty gritty today.  Literally, in fact – we started off the morning by separating ancient microscopic fossils from the mud and clay they were buried in.  It was actually a pretty lengthy procedure.  After we extracted the fossils, we looked at them through several different types of microscopes and tried to identify them as well as we could.  I’ll post some pix tomorrow; they’re saved on a different computer for now.

 

In the afternoon, we hit the books.  Our goal was to learn how scientists correlate dates, fossils, and ocean sediments.  It’s a pretty tricky process, but with a little coaching, we were all able to do some interpretation on our own.  We did most of the work in the conference room (pictured below).

 

 

Tomorrow, we get to sleep in!  (Class starts at 8:00 instead of 7:30.)  I plan on getting up super early so I can go work out and have a HUGE breakfast of raw squid and pickles.  Mmm!

 

Day 5:  Thursday, November 3, 2005

 

What a great weather day!  The wind was blowing at 40 knots (that’s about 50 mph for you landlubbers) and the waves were 15-20 feet high!  Here’s a picture.  Try not to get sick.

 

 

But, it wasn’t all fun and games.  This morning we spent a good deal of time examining ancient sediments that had been drilled from the ocean floor.  We learned some of the tricks geologists use to figure out how old sediments are, and we looked at several different types of fossils and learned how the composition of the sediment often is dependent on the types of critters that live in the water column.

 

This afternoon, we participated in the first of many expected curriculum workshops.  We hammered out some of the details regarding how we want our activities to look and feel.  On Saturday, the plan is to start pounding out the activities (we haven’t quite figured out how to approach that yet; that’s what tomorrow is for).

Day 2:  Monday, October 31, 2005

 

Today, after breakfast, we finally got on the ship.  It’s pretty big and confusing.  It would be a great place to play hide-and-seek.  However, if I hid, I can’t imagine that anyone would be particularly motivated to find me.

 

This morning we got our cabin assignments, then started off with some safety information and a brief tour of some of the places on the ship.  Then we had a scavenger hunt, which my team and I are proud to say that we won!  However, there weren’t any prizes.  So I went back to my cabin and sulked.  Then I proceeded to fall asleep and miss 15 minutes of the afternoon lecture.  I am an embarrassment!  Nobody seemed that upset, but I did notice that I no longer have an assigned seat on the lifeboats.  I’m hoping that they’ll at least tow me behind them in an inner tube.

 

We just finished dinner; now we’ve got a couple more lessons/lectures/activities before hitting the sack.  Just think – tomorrow, at this time, I’ll be puking over the side of the ship!
